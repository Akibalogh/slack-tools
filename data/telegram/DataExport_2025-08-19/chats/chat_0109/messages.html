In terms of redemption risk, the dlcBTC merchant system is perhaps most closely analogous to the system of banks backing USDC.

USDC is backed by USD and cash equivalents held in bank accounts. These banks, generally Tier 1 institutions such as SVB, Wells Fargo, and others, do not hold cash in a fungible, physical form. Instead, they hold bank deposits, which are essentially numbers within the bank's proprietary systems. Access to these reserves is controlled by designated officers with the necessary security clearance. In the event of a bankruptcy, control over these reserves (and the keys to access them) transfers to a bankruptcy court or a government entity.

This structure introduces a level of counterparty risk, as seen when SVB's failure led to a temporary de-pegging of USDC due to lost consumer confidence. However, once confidence was restored, USDC's value recovered.

The temporary depeg of USDC created an arbitrage opportunity, where funds capitalized on the situation by purchasing USDC for under $1 and selling it once its value rebounded. This arbitrage play highlights the market's ability to identify and exploit price discrepancies between the perceived and actual value of the asset. The swift restoration of USDC's peg to the USD demonstrates not only the resilience of stablecoin mechanisms in the face of financial disturbances but also the potential for strategic gains through timely market actions.

dlcBTC uses Bitcoin that is physically locked using DLCs, thereby eliminating the traditional counterparty risk associated with bank deposits. This physical locking mechanism ensures that the BTC remains present and accounted for, irrespective of the financial health of the dlcBTC merchant. Therefore, the risk of a dlcBTC merchant facing bankruptcy does not directly impact the presence or availability of the locked BTC, although it could potentially affect consumer confidence and thus the peg of dlcBTC to BTC.

Hi Dae, there are indeed pros and cons to allocating incentives based on a fixed token price assumption. On the positive side, this approach is more predictable and easier for project teams to manage their budget and token allocation. However, the downside is that given the current market conditions, the assumed token price tends to be on the lower side, increasing the risk of overpayment, as you mentioned.

From our observations of other projects' incentive programs, a fixed price assumption remains more popular. This is because the TWAP approach, while potentially fairer, introduces additional risks if the token price opens lower than expected.

One hybrid approach we've seen involves combining both strategies: paying out a higher fixed yield based on the TWAP price, but also implementing a cap on the maximum number of tokens allocated for incentives. This method can balance predictability with market responsiveness.
