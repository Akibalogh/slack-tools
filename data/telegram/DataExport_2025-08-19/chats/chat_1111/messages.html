tBTC (and the upcoming sBTC) use federated networks of validators. Bitcoiners are hesitant to lock into these external validators since it‚Äôs unclear what the risks are. For example, tBTC has an insurance pool to cover any overdrafts.. but in the case of a bank run, nobody knows how that would play out. So, it‚Äôs probably fine for smaller amounts but institutions would be very wary to take that level of risk.

With DLCs, the Bitcoin is locked directly on Bitcoin chain. We don‚Äôt use validators, and deposits are secured by the full hashrate of the Bitcoin network. So, we‚Äôre targeting institutions from the get-go.
22:15

AB
07:22
Aki Balogh
In our original understanding of DLCs, a protocol like Maple was to be the counterparty. So the thinking was, if Maple is hacked and the attestors are hacked, BTC is moved to Maple and stolen there.

However in the last two weeks, ABCDE capital has convinced us to simplify the system and just produce a dlcBTC token. The depositor locks into a DLC. We function as the counterparty. There is a liquidation address where BTC goes in a "liquidation" scenario, but it's a third-party custodian.

In the new model, our understanding is that there will be an Emergency DAO that can flush the system in case of an emergency. The biggest risk with us currently is that we're a new protocol and there's a chance that a smart contract bug results in funds being locked forever. So, the Emergency DAO can vote to trigger the liquidation outcome and flush all BTC to a central location, where it can be redistributed to the users.

That means that the attack vectors are even lower. Someone would have to hack the Emergency DAO to get voting power to change the liquidation address and to trigger liquidations. But the more distributed that the DAO is, the less likely that this is to happen.

Anyway, in all scenarios, this is much safer than wBTC or tBTC. Both of those pool BTC assets. With a DLC, each DLC functions as an individual on-chain escrow, so funds are not pooled anywhere.
