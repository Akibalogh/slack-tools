Aki Balogh
Hey Karim!

The key distinction of a DLC is that only the original depositor can ever receive funds. So, if we have only one merchant (e.g. FalconX), and retail buys or swaps for dlcBTC, then only FalconX can ever redeem the dlcBTC. This gives them a lot of power, i.e. they could refuse to honor redemptions.

The "other signer" of the multisig is actually our protocol and the attestor network. The key feature is that we can never receive funds, since the depositor "pre-signs" one payout address which points back to them (which is what we call self-wrapping).
19:11
The dlcBTC merchant is the depositor (e.g. FalconX), so there is no risk of insolvency, just the risk of them not redeeming the dlcBTC. For example, if they go into bankruptcy, then everyone's stuck with dlcBTC for years
19:13
So, our thinking is that the counterparty risk of dlcBTC is the composite risk of all underling dlcBTC merchants. If you hold dlcBTC, your counterparties are all of the FalconX'es of the world. For example, if FalconX holds 5% of all dlcBTC and they go bankrupt, then only 95% of the dlcBTC can be redeemed
19:14
That said, we are open to broadening it out ‚Äî perhaps even retail could mint dlcBTC. But then the risk is probably higher. For ex, retail could lose their private keys, making it impossible for them to ever redeem dlcBTC. This could dry up liquidity in an adverse market event
19:14
In reply to this message
For this, we took inspiration from the wBTC merchant network, which works the same way. wBTC merchants can make 0.25% swap fees for providing this service
