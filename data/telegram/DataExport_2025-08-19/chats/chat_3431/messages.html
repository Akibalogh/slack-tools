Aki Balogh
Discreet Log Contracts (DLCs) achieve atomicity in cross-chain transactions by using cryptographic techniques to predefine the potential outcomes of the transaction before it's executed. This allows participants to agree on the possible outcomes without needing to trust an intermediary or each other.

Here's how it generally works:
1. Setup Phase: Participants create a multisignature (multisig) address, into which they both deposit funds. They also collaboratively create a set of Conditional Execution Transactions (CETs) that cover all predefined outcomes of the contract.
2. Commitment Phase: Both parties commit to the CETs and share necessary cryptographic signatures, ensuring that they have full knowledge of the outcomes and that neither party can unilaterally alter the contract.
3. Execution Phase: When the condition specified in the contract is met (e.g., a price reaching a certain level), one of the CETs is executed, and the transaction is atomically finalized. The funds are automatically distributed according to the agreed-upon outcome.
4. Attestation: In cross-chain scenarios, attestors (trusted oracles or decentralized networks of attestors) might be used to verify the outcome on the other chain, ensuring that the condition was met according to the agreement. Attestors sign the outcome without knowing the details of the contract, adding another layer of trust-minimization.

This process ensures that either the transaction is executed according to the agreed terms or not at all, hence the atomicity. It removes the need for intermediaries and minimizes trust, making it ideal for secure, decentralized cross-chain transactions.
