I deeply understand and respect the choices of users, and I believe it is crucial. Given Ledger's reputation, it is reasonable for many users to choose Ledger right away.
However, there is an important piece of information I would like to share with both of you, which may also be important for your clients' decision-making.
Please allow me to explain in a bit more detail, which may take a few minutes of your time to read.

In May of this year, Ledger released a new service called “Ledger Recovery” , which essentially splits a user's Seed Phrase into three parts and uploads them to three different institutions for safekeeping, with the user's consent after upgrading device firmware and Ledger Live(Software). If the user can provide two of the Seed Phrase parts, Ledger will restore their data. The original intention of this service was to prevent users from losing their Seed Phrase and thus losing access to their assets. However, since its launch, it has stirred up a great controversy worldwide, with the majority of users believing that the Seed Phrase should be self-contained, and if entrusted to others, it is tantamount to handing over ownership of the assets.
At the same time, it should be noted that according to Ledger's announcement, users need to upgrade the firmware and Ledger Live to a relatively new version and agree to the service before they can use it. However, from a technical perspective, once the firmware and Ledger Live are upgraded, the conditions for enabling this service are met.

We believe that Ledger would not proactively attempt to obtain a user's Seed Phrase.
However, many users think that, according to Ledger's description, Ledger does indeed have the capability to obtain a user's Seed Phrase after upgrading the firmware. To give an example, it's like leaving a backdoor at the device level after the upgrade, and in fact, Ledger can access the user's Seed Phrase at any time, depending on whether they choose to access it. Consequently, after the launch of this service, many users worldwide have expressed great concerns as this service contradicts the fundamental spirit of blockchain. It has not been even a year since the incident with FTX, and everyone still clearly realizes that there are no companies in the cryptocurrency field that are too big to go bankrupt. Rather than trusting a specific company, it is better to trust the code because only open-source code does not lie
At the same time, they believe that if one day Ledger is compelled by a government to provide users' Seed Phrases, they can technically do it. This might be a risk for users who have a particularly large amount of assets.
And if users want to use the latest features on Ledger or trade on the latest public chain, they must upgrade their device firmware and Ledger Live. Therefore, users cannot avoid such risks by not upgrading the firmware.

Therefore, users from Germany, Brazil, the United States, Japan, and other countries have been actively searching for alternative hardware wallet products to replace Ledger, and ultimately, a considerable number of users have chosen OneKey.
A Well-known German influencer named Hoss has specially introduced OneKey in his YouTube videos, emphasizing the openness of OneKey products.
The largest crypto community in Japan, KudasaiJP, held a Twitter Space event with OneKey in June, and many members of that community have become loyal users of OneKey.
There are many reports available online for further information.

As I mentioned before, we believe that Ledger will not proactively do anything malicious. Ledger's products still have their great features. If users do not mind entrusting their Seed Phrase to others, then this issue will not be a problem for them. On the other hand, if users only learn about this information after choosing Ledger, it may lead to concerns about the security of their assets, which could then potentially affect their confidence in participating in DeFi with BTC or even result in a new demand to switch hardware wallet partners.

Thank you for sharing. Although I haven't finished reading the entire article, based on the abstract and some suggestions mentioned for improving security, I believe we can roughly understand the author's perspective.

As of now, almost all browser extension wallets and software wallets face the risk of Seedphrase leakage. This is because the majority of personal PCs and smartphones are currently unable to withstand targeted attacks by hackers. A Trojan virus can easily take control of such electronic devices, granting hackers access to all files on the device, including browser and device cache folders, photo albums, etc. Seedphrases are often stored in these folders. Institutional users, despite employing higher-level security strategies, may resist conventional network attacks, but they are not absolutely secure against more targeted attacks, especially considering the possibility of human error during the process (such as the asset theft incident in the 2022 Roning chain cross-chain bridge, where hackers may have used social engineering methods).

The author suggests one solution in the article is to integrate a hardware wallet module into the device. This might be a future solution, but ensuring that the module can operate independently after a device is attacked, avoiding remote access to its internal data, and preventing signature operations through electronic means (such as controlling the strength of electric current) will also be a challenge. At least for now, a complete process where electronic devices exist independently, use secure chips to store and call Seedphrases and Private Keys for verification, and confirm operations through user confirmation from external sources is secure and effective for the majority of users.

Of course, hardware wallets are not foolproof, and users still need to address some issues during use, such as the physical offline storage of mnemonic phrases and prompt responses to device loss. However, at least for now, hardware wallets are indeed the best solution accessible to the majority of users in addressing asset security issues, whether in terms of cost-effectiveness, ease of operation, or security.

We look forward to more simple, convenient, and secure solutions in the future with the further development of security technologies to help safeguard our encrypted assets.
