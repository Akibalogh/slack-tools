The Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) Regulation, effective in the European Union, establishes a harmonized framework for regulating crypto-assets, including Asset-Referenced Tokens (ARTs) and Electronic Money Tokens (EMTs), which are considered types of stablecoins. Below, I outline the key aspects of ARTs under MiCA and the specific application of the regulation to algorithmic stablecoins like DAI and LUSD.

---

### Asset-Referenced Tokens (ARTs) under MiCA

ARTs are crypto-assets that aim to maintain a stable value by referencing a basket of assets, such as multiple currencies, commodities, or other crypto-assets. Unlike EMTs, which are pegged to a single official currency (e.g., USD or EUR), ARTs derive their value from a combination of assets. Key regulatory requirements for ARTs include:

1. Authorization:
- Issuers of ARTs must be legal entities established in the EU and obtain authorization from a national competent authority (NCA) unless the ART is offered solely to qualified investors or the total value of the ARTs is below ‚Ç¨5 million over a 12-month period.[](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/mica-regulation-new-regulatory-framework-crypto-assets-issuers-and-crypto-asset)[](https://www.dechert.com/knowledge/onpoint/2024/6/mica-phase-one--issuers-of-arts-and-emts-subject-to-authorizatio.html)
- Credit institutions issuing ARTs are exempt from authorization but must notify the NCA 90 working days before issuance.[](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/mica-regulation-new-regulatory-framework-crypto-assets-issuers-and-crypto-asset)

2. Reserve Requirements:
- Issuers must maintain a reserve of assets to back the ART‚Äôs value, protecting against market and currency risks. These reserves must be securely managed, segregated from the issuer‚Äôs own assets, and held in custody by a third party.[](https://swissborg.com/blog/mica-regulation-stablecoins)[](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/mica-regulation-new-regulatory-framework-crypto-assets-issuers-and-crypto-asset)
- Reserves cannot be pledged as collateral, ensuring liquidity and stability.[](https://swissborg.com/blog/mica-regulation-stablecoins)

3. White Paper:
- Issuers must publish a detailed white paper, approved by the NCA, outlining the token‚Äôs stabilization mechanism, investment policies, custody arrangements, and risks.[](https://www.dechert.com/knowledge/onpoint/2024/6/mica-phase-one--issuers-of-arts-and-emts-subject-to-authorizatio.html)[](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2024/07/alerts-finance-ftec-are-you-ready-for-mica-implementation)

4. Transparency and Reporting:
- Issuers must provide regular transparency reports on reserve backing and undergo independent audits to verify asset segregation and security.[](https://legalnodes.com/article/mica-regulation-explained)[](https://ec.europa.eu/newsroom/fisma/items/754690/en)
- For ARTs deemed ‚Äúsignificant‚Äù (e.g., based on market cap, transaction volume, or number of holders), stricter oversight by the European Banking Authority (EBA) applies, including higher capital requirements and interoperability rules.[](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/2cec201e/regulating-crypto-assets-in-europe-practical-guide-to-mica)[](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=3b478651-6034-4ca1-8089-8e8b9bcf34f5)
07:05
5. Transaction Caps:
- ARTs used widely as a means of exchange are subject to a cap of 1 million transactions or ‚Ç¨200 million in daily value to mitigate risks to financial stability.[](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=6e684632-ad4b-419b-a7ab-7866ae3b1513)[](https://treasuryxl.com/blog/mica-regulation-for-stablecoins-standard-for-worldwide-application/)

6. Consumer Protection:
- MiCA imposes strict marketing rules to prevent misleading practices and ensures investors receive reliable information.[](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2024/07/alerts-finance-ftec-are-you-ready-for-mica-implementation)
- Issuers are liable for losses due to malfunctions or cyberattacks.[](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=3b478651-6034-4ca1-8089-8e8b9bcf34f5)

### Algorithmic Stablecoins (e.g., DAI and LUSD) under MiCA

Algorithmic stablecoins, such as DAI (issued by MakerDAO/Sky) and LUSD (issued by Liquity), aim to maintain a stable value through algorithms that adjust token supply based on market demand, often without direct asset backing or by using decentralized collateral (e.g., ETH for DAI). The application of MiCA to these tokens is complex due to their decentralized and algorithmic nature. Here‚Äôs how MiCA addresses them:

1. Classification Challenges:
- MiCA does not explicitly define ‚Äústablecoins‚Äù but categorizes them as ARTs or EMTs. Algorithmic stablecoins are generally not considered EMTs, as they do not reference a single official currency.[](https://academic.oup.com/cmlj/article/17/3/365/6609567)[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)
- However, MiCA‚Äôs definition of ARTs includes tokens that maintain stable value ‚Äúirrespective of how the issuer intends to design the crypto-asset, including the mechanism to maintain a stable value.‚Äù This suggests that algorithmic stablecoins referencing multiple assets (e.g., DAI‚Äôs multi-collateral system with ETH, USDC, and treasuries) could be classified as ARTs.[](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=6e684632-ad4b-419b-a7ab-7866ae3b1513)[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)
- If an algorithmic stablecoin does not reference any assets (e.g., purely algorithmic like Ampleforth), it may fall outside the ART or EMT categories and be treated as a general crypto-asset under Title II, subject to lighter transparency rules (e.g., white paper requirements but no authorization).[](https://academic.oup.com/cmlj/article/17/3/365/6609567)[](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=6e684632-ad4b-419b-a7ab-7866ae3b1513)
07:05
2. Decentralized Stablecoins (DAI and LUSD):
- DAI: DAI is a decentralized stablecoin backed by a mix of crypto-assets (e.g., ETH, USDC) and managed via smart contracts to maintain a USD peg. Its decentralized nature, governed by MakerDAO (now Sky), complicates MiCA compliance because MiCA primarily targets tokens with identifiable issuers.[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)
- A legal report commissioned by Sky argued that DAI (and its successor, USDS) does not fall under MiCA‚Äôs EMT or ART clauses because it lacks an identifiable issuer, meaning EU exchanges could list it without requiring the issuer to register as an electronic money institution (EMI).[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)
- However, this interpretation is debated. An EU advisor involved in MiCA‚Äôs drafting stated that exchanges can only list regulated EMTs or ARTs, and courts may need to resolve this ambiguity.[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)
- Some exchanges (e.g., Binance, Coinbase) have already restricted or delisted DAI for EEA users due to uncertainty about its compliance.[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)[](https://treasuryxl.com/blog/mica-regulation-for-stablecoins-standard-for-worldwide-application/)
- LUSD: LUSD, issued by Liquity, is another decentralized stablecoin backed by ETH collateral and stabilized through algorithmic mechanisms. Like DAI, its decentralized structure raises questions about whether it has an identifiable issuer under MiCA. There is limited specific information on LUSD‚Äôs status, but it likely faces similar challenges as DAI, potentially being classified as an ART if it references multiple assets or as a general crypto-asset if purely algorithmic.[](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-74889-9_8)
07:05
3. Regulatory Challenges for Algorithmic Stablecoins:
- Authorization and EU Presence: MiCA requires ART issuers to be EU-based legal entities, which is problematic for decentralized protocols like MakerDAO/Sky or Liquity, which operate via decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs). Complying with authorization requirements may be infeasible due to their non-custodial, decentralized nature.[](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/overview-eu-markets-crypto-assets-mica-regulation-part-peter-howitt)[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)
- Reserve Requirements: Algorithmic stablecoins often rely on over-collateralization (e.g., ETH for DAI) rather than liquid reserves like fiat or treasuries. MiCA‚Äôs requirement for 1:1 liquid asset backing may not align with their design, potentially disqualifying them as ARTs or requiring significant operational changes.[](https://www.sanctions.io/blog/the-eus-mica-regulation-for-crypto-explained)[](https://www.sygna.io/blog/what-is-mica-markets-in-crypto-assets-eu-regulation-guide/)
- Potential Ban or Restrictions: Early MiCA proposals suggested a ban on algorithmic stablecoins due to risks highlighted by events like the Terra/LUNA collapse. While the final text does not explicitly ban them, it imposes strict reserve and transparency rules that algorithmic stablecoins may struggle to meet. Some sources indicate a move toward restricting algorithmic stablecoins in the EU.[](https://www.sanctions.io/blog/the-eus-mica-regulation-for-crypto-explained)[](https://www.sygna.io/blog/what-is-mica-markets-in-crypto-assets-eu-regulation-guide/)
- Exchange Obligations: If DAI or LUSD lacks an identifiable issuer, MiCA places the burden on crypto-asset service providers (CASPs), such as exchanges, to ensure compliance (e.g., by preparing white papers and warning users of risks). This has led some exchanges to delist non-compliant stablecoins to avoid regulatory penalties.[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)[](https://treasuryxl.com/blog/mica-regulation-for-stablecoins-standard-for-worldwide-application/)

4. Current Status and Market Impact:
- As of June 30, 2024, MiCA‚Äôs stablecoin rules (Titles III and IV) are in effect, requiring ART and EMT issuers to be authorized. Non-compliant stablecoins, including DAI, face restrictions in the EEA.[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)[](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2024/07/alerts-finance-ftec-are-you-ready-for-mica-implementation)
- Major exchanges like Uphold, Binance, and Kraken have delisted stablecoins like DAI and Tether (USDT) for EEA users, while Circle‚Äôs USDC has achieved MiCA compliance through EMI authorization.[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)[](https://treasuryxl.com/blog/mica-regulation-for-stablecoins-standard-for-worldwide-application/)[](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2024/07/alerts-finance-ftec-are-you-ready-for-mica-implementation)
- There is little public information on whether MakerDAO/Sky or Liquity plans to pursue MiCA compliance (e.g., by obtaining an EMI license or restructuring operations).[](https://coincodex.com/article/43554/mica-stablecoins/)
- The decentralized nature of DAI and LUSD may allow them to operate outside MiCA‚Äôs scope if they are deemed to lack an issuer, but this depends on regulatory interpretation and potential court rulings.[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)
07:05
5. DeFi and MiCA:
- MiCA‚Äôs initial framework explicitly sidestepped decentralized finance (DeFi), focusing on centralized issuers. However, decentralized stablecoins like DAI and LUSD blur the line, as their governance (e.g., DAOs) may be scrutinized for centralization.[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)[](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/overview-eu-markets-crypto-assets-mica-regulation-part-peter-howitt)
- MiCA‚Äôs recital notes that crypto-assets without an identifiable issuer (common in DeFi) are exempt from Titles II, III, and IV, but CASPs offering such tokens must still comply with transparency and risk disclosure rules. This creates a gray area for DAI and LUSD.[](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/dai-sky-stablecoins-are-controversial-under-mica-regulations-implications-for-tether/)
