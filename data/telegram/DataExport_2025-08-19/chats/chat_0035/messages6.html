### How Individuals Maintain Their Wallet Balances
* Self-Custodial Balances:
Each participant‚Äôs wallet contains a local state, represented cryptographically as a node in a hierarchical Sparse Merkle Tree (SMT) structure. This structure ensures that every user independently maintains and verifies their wallet balance without relying on centralized systems.
* Wallet States in the SMT:
* A wallet state includes:
* Balances of channels the wallet owns.
* Metadata for these channels.
* A nonce to ensure unique transitions and prevent replay attacks.
* These states are organized as follows:
* Channel SMT: Tracks individual channel states (e.g., balances, metadata).
* Wallet SMT: Summarizes all channels owned by a wallet.
* Global SMT: Summarizes all wallet roots into a single root, which is periodically anchored on Bitcoin.
* Independent Control:
* Users independently update their wallet balances through cryptographically signed state transitions with other participants (e.g., sending or receiving funds in a channel).
* These updates are not broadcast on Bitcoin. Instead, participants exchange the updated state (e.g., balance changes, nonce increments), which they can verify using Merkle proofs.
* Trustless Verification:
* At any time, a participant can recompute their balance using their wallet‚Äôs local state and verify its correctness by comparing the Merkle proof with the root hash anchored on Bitcoin. This eliminates the need to ‚Äúpull‚Äù balances from a central source.

### How Settlement Back to Bitcoin Works
1. Anchoring State:

2. Initiating Settlement:

* They submit their latest signed state and Merkle proof to Bitcoin.
* The proof verifies that their wallet‚Äôs state is part of the anchored SMT root.

3. Settlement to Bitcoin:
* Once the proof is validated:
* The user‚Äôs balance is withdrawn directly to their Bitcoin address.
* Channels associated with the wallet are closed, and the corresponding funds are unlocked.
4. Fallback Mechanism:

BitVM Relies on Optimistic Verification:
BitVM operates under the assumption that transactions are valid unless actively challenged during a dispute window. LPs using a BitVM-based bridge would need to rely on these optimistic assumptions to ensure the correctness of their transactions and the integrity of the bridge. Swappers cannot opt out of these assumptions if they interact with the same bridge.

Arch is Deterministic:
Arch ties all state transitions and executions directly to Bitcoin Layer 1 transactions. This eliminates the need for dispute windows, or optimistic mechanisms, ensuring trustless execution for all participants. LPs and swappers using Arch rollup style apps, operate in the same trustless environment, without different layers of trust.

In BitVM-based systems, the bridge is central to the minting and burning of assets (e.g., wrapped BTC). LPs lock assets into the bridge to provide liquidity, and swappers rely on the same bridge to exchange assets or redeem them. This makes it impossible for LPs to use the bridge while swappers do not, as all participants are dependent on the bridge for asset interactions.
