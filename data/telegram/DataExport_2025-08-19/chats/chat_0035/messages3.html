Notes

A question I‚Äôve been thinking about for the last few days is if the sequencer(s) and the bridge operator(s), in a BitVM-style rollup construct, are the same parties.

Sims says they‚Äôre not (at least in their design). Sequencer would have a similar role to those in Ethereum. Takes transactions, batches them and posts the batches along with a proof of execution to the L1.

The bridge program is a separate entity in this construct. Their responsible for verifying state transitions and corresponding validity proofs so they can permit BTC withdrawals for users exiting the rollup.

In this example, withdrawal requests go to the sequencer. A number of requests can be aggregated. These requests are included in a state transition with a corresponding SNARK validity proof. The reason this program needs to be able to verify a SNARK is so that it can verify withdrawal requests against the most recent state transition that the rollup proposed.

The protocol can select one of the bridge operators to process the withdrawal and then handle a reimbursement to themselves after some period of time. Only one of the bridge operators in this construction (1-N) needs to be honest to 1) process withdrawals and 2) maintain the honesty of the peg.

1) is because you need at least one party to be willing to front the liquidity to process withdrawals.

2) is to ensure that there is a mechanism to dispute fraudulent withdrawals processed by other parties.

The rollup can exist without the bridge. The rollup can inherit double spend security simply by sending sequencer batches and validity proofs to Bitcoin and have the rollup nodes agree on the state transition. The bridge program allows us to get Bitcoin onto the rollup in a more trust-minimized fashion.
