Ham Kayan
Founder Considerations for Cash Management:

With the uncertainty created with the failure of the Silicon Valley Bank, and the continued micromanagement by government and over-regulation of banks, founders need to consider a more diversified cash management approach. ¬†

Since boards and management are responsible for making payroll, even in times of crisis, it is important to build out contingency plans for bank failures that could happen more and more often if government continues to print money and whipsaw interest rates to counteract inflation caused by the over-printing of money.

The past week showed us the importance of having a sound strategy and contingency plans in the face of cash uncertainty. Many startups came to us for emergency relief when Silicon Valley Bank and other banks shut down. In order to avoid desperate measures or even total collapse in the future, we advocate the following plan. ¬†

1. Keep it simple. Your primary responsibility to shareholders is to build a business, and cash is the business‚Äôs life blood. ¬†

2. Diversify risk. Businesses can no longer rely on one bank or one governing body to manage their cash. We recommend keeping at least 6 months of short-term cash in each of two banks, one local bank and one global bank, and at least two payrolls worth of cash in Bitcoin or other crypto currencies. Excess cash can be longer term, but easily saleable in emergencies. For the first time in many years, governments are taking over banks and governments themselves are at risk of becoming insolvent. Bitcoin is a hedge against a ‚Äúdomino‚Äù run on the banks and on poor over-controlling governance.

3. Yield and capital appreciation. It has been years since yield has been a major factor in cash management, since interest rates and inflation were so low during the productivity boom that came from free and trusting governance. But now, we have both high interest rates and inflation, so awareness of risk and return on a company‚Äôs cash can be mission critical. Normally a company‚Äôs treasury department is mostly meant to preserve cash, but these are not normal times.

4. Awareness of fraud. When uncertainty and complications from complex regulations occur, fraudsters can find weaknesses in a system and exploit them. You might find your team so busy filling out compliance forms that they miss a bank hack or wire fraud. Time is of the essence when you are hacked. Be prepared to have a ‚Äúdrop everything and all hands on deck‚Äù event in the case of fraud.

5. Awareness of customers‚Äô and suppliers‚Äô vulnerabilities. The best businesses have healthy businesses working with them. Have frank and honest conversations with your key accounts to understand vulnerabilities that might have a detrimental effect on your business.

6. Verify anomalies and process changes. Check with all parties involved when you notice a change in wire instructions or a new approval system. To avoid phishing theft, call your direct contacts, not the numbers in the email.

7. Decentralize. With so many people working from home, decisions may need to be made without sticking to the corporate ladder. Set up redundancy in your business to move more quickly. ¬†

Special thanks to individuals at The Wharton School for their recommendations that helped us think about this.
