The main difference between DLCs using our Attestation network and a bridge such as tBTC is that the user does not lock BTC into our Attestors. Alice and Bob's BTC addresses are locked in at DLC creation and cannot be changed. The BTC is kept on-chain throughout. So, if the system is hacked, BTC locked in previous DLCs cannot be transferred to an arbitrary third-party address.

A few possible attack vectors that we discussed:
* If the depositor (Alice) hacks the attestation network and publishes incorrect repayment signals, they can run away with the BTC. However, they will only receive their BTC deposits -- they cannot access others'. (If such an attack were to happen, presumably the lending protocol can pause their vaults.)
* If the counterparty (Bob) is hacked, and the entire attestation network is hacked, the BTC deposits can be moved to the counterparty, where it could be stolen. They would not be able to steal others' deposits, but they could access BTC in DLCs where the counterparty is a signer.
* Also, if a hacker hacks the attestors and the counterparty, then new deposits would be at risk of theft. The attestor would publish a liquidation outcome and steal funds from the counterparty. However, the time window for this would be relatively short -- e.g., assume that hacks are detected within a 3-day window, during which lending protocols could pause deposits.
* If we lose private keys to our attestors, then those DLCs could timeout or remain frozen forever. This risk is common across many DeFi protocols. To mitigate this, we are implementing top-tier key safety measures, such as the use of HSMs and private key safety services from Coinbase Custody or from BitGo.

A few benefits of DLCs:

The attestor does not know who the parties are, or which transactions they're attesting to, so a vast majority of attestors would need to be hacked. For example, in an M-of-N setup (950-of-1000 attestors), virtually all attestors would need to be hacked to approach 100% of DLCs closed.

Further, the attestors have a back-checker component that verifies the Ethereum signal on-chain before publishing an attestation, and Alice and Bob's wallets can independently verify the signals as well.
